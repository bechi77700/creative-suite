# Static Ad Concept Library

Reference catalogue describing common static ad concepts seen on
Meta Ads. Use as creative vocabulary and inspiration when generating
prompts. Output format remains the Nanobanana structure defined
elsewhere in the system prompt — this document does NOT override
generation rules, brand knowledge, or static ad references. It
enriches Claude's understanding of which formats exist and what
each one means.

When the user has selected a specific concept, materialize that
concept across all prompts in the batch (with creative variation
inside it). When no concept is selected, treat this catalogue as
background culture — choose freely.

---

# Annotated Product

Product is the hero of the frame, surrounded by short text labels,
callouts, or features pulled out around it. Information-dense,
educational. Common in D2C verticals with multi-feature products.

## Arrows
Product centered, arrows pointing outward to short feature labels
(2-6 words each). Arrow style sets tone: clean vector = tech,
hand-drawn marker = authentic. 3-6 arrows max.
**Best for**: tech, supplements, ergonomic gear, multi-component
products. **Tone**: educational, demonstrative.

## Bullet Points
Product on one side, vertical list of 3-6 benefits with green
checkmarks (✓) on the other. Often in a colored panel. Sometimes
mixed (✓ for us / ✗ for competitors). "Value stack" framing common.
**Best for**: feature-rich products, comparison-friendly categories,
supplements. **Tone**: rational, no-nonsense.

## Classic
Clean product shot, minimal annotation, one short headline OR no
copy. Controlled lighting, neutral or branded background. The
product carries the ad. Often a small trust badge in the corner.
**Best for**: premium positioning, visually striking products,
warm audiences. **Tone**: confident, polished, restrained.

# Comparison / Split

Frame divided into two states / sides / moments. Contrast carries
the persuasion — the eye reads "two things, one is better"
pre-attentively. High-converting on cold traffic.

## Problem / Solution
Two-panel split: problem side (muted, frowning, red highlights) vs
solution side (bright, calm, product visible). Sometimes with arrow
between them.
**Best for**: clearly visualizable daily frictions (back pain, sleep,
mess, leaks). **Tone**: empathetic, relief-driven.

## Split + Benefit
Vertical split: product hero on one side, 3-5 benefits (often with
icons) on the other. Equal visual weight to both halves. Outcome-
framed benefits ("Sleep deeper") work better than features.
**Best for**: considered purchases, supplements, devices, skincare.
**Tone**: balanced, confident.

## Split + Offer
Same structure as Split + Benefit but the second half is the OFFER:
discount %, strikethrough old price, promo code, BOGO, bundle. Loud
colors (red, neon yellow), bold heavy type. Often urgency cues.
**Best for**: scaling winning creatives, retargeting warm audiences,
clearance. **Tone**: urgent, transactional.

## Before / After
Same subject in two states separated by time and the product. Same
angle, same framing, same lighting. Often "Day 1 / Day 30" or
"Week 1 / Week 4". Real-looking photos (not stock) crucial.
Outcome-led copy with specific numbers.
**Best for**: visible-result categories (skincare, weight loss,
fitness, posture, cleaning). **Tone**: hopeful, transformation-driven.

## Us vs Them
Two-column comparison chart: brand on one side with green ✓, generic
competitor on the other with red ✗. 5-8 attribute rows. Headers
"Us / Them" or "[Brand] / Other brands" or "New way / Old way".
**Best for**: positioning against incumbents, fact-based
differentiation. **Tone**: confident, slightly confrontational.

# Social Proof

Persuasion comes from other people, not the brand. Customer reviews,
ratings, testimonials, peer voices. Bypasses ad-skepticism. Higher
conversion when proof feels close to "real person" vs polished
testimonial.

## Trustpilot
Trustpilot rating screenshot or rendering as the centerpiece: 5
yellow-green stars, Trustpilot logo, rating score (4.8 / 5), review
count. Sometimes paired with one excerpted review or a "trust wall"
of badges (GMP, CE, ISO).
**Best for**: brands with genuinely high ratings, trust-driven
categories (supplements, beauty), retargeting. **Tone**: institutional,
credible.

## Reviews (Avis)
Single customer review presented as a clean review card: 5 stars,
first name + initial, 1-3 sentences, verified-buyer badge. Card
overlaid on product shot or clean background. Conversational
language, specific outcome mentioned.
**Best for**: emotional / transformation results, building credibility
for unfamiliar brands, warm audiences. **Tone**: personal, authentic.

## Multi-reviews
Grid of 3-6 review cards together — implies "many people, same
verdict." Cards often slightly tilted/overlapped to feel organic.
Mix of names, ages, genders for diversity. Aggregate rating shown.
**Best for**: established brands with real review base, retargeting,
high-objection categories. **Tone**: cumulative, validating.

## Realistic Reviews
Designed to look NOT like an ad: mimics Amazon review, Facebook post,
Instagram comment, iPhone DM screenshot, SMS. Casual tone, lowercase,
emojis, real-life product photo attached. Native UI accuracy is
crucial.
**Best for**: cold traffic fatigued on polished ads, Gen Z, peer-
recommendation categories. **Tone**: native, candid, off-the-cuff.

# Offer

The OFFER is the hero of the frame, not the product or benefit.
Best used on warm or returning audiences who already understand the
product's value. Cold traffic rarely converts on offer alone.

## Offer Forward
Discount, price, code or deal in massive bold type (30-50% of frame
height). Loud contrasting colors (red, neon yellow). Strikethrough
old price → bold new price. Promo code in contrast box. Urgency line
("48h only"). Product secondary.
**Best for**: scaling winners during sales, retargeting warm
audiences, clearance, intro pricing. **Tone**: urgent, transactional.

## Visible Bulk
Many units of the product visible in the frame — stacks, multipacks,
hauls, bundles. Wide or top-down angle to maximize quantity. Hand
for scale. Implies value through abundance, not through %.
**Best for**: subscription products, bundles, multipacks, value-
conscious audiences, gift sets. **Tone**: generous, "look what you
get."

# Handwriting

Text that looks written by a human hand — on paper, on the body, on
post-its, in amateur tools. Bypasses the "this is an ad" filter.
Real handwriting outperforms handwriting fonts.

## Paper / Notebook
Handwritten note on paper / notebook / lined page, photographed with
the product on a real surface. Black or blue ballpoint. Often
slightly crumpled. Sometimes signed with a first name. Founder-letter
or customer-testimonial framing.
**Best for**: founder-led brand stories, testimonials, closing
announcements, promo codes that feel like insider tips. **Tone**:
personal, sincere.

## Body Writing
Text rendered directly ON a person's body — usually the body part
tied to the problem (back, foot, stomach, scar, knee). Outcome-led
all-caps text ("12 ANS DE DOULEUR DISPARUE", "VENTRE DE BIÈRE
DISPARU"). Real photo, natural lighting.
**Best for**: pain-relief, weight loss, skincare with visible
improvement, body-located issues. **Tone**: visceral, embodied.

## Paint / Amateur
Deliberately created in MS Paint, KolourPaint, Comic Sans. Crude
execution IS the message. Pattern-interrupt by being visibly ugly.
Often self-aware copy ("Our designer is on maternity leave"). Often
promo-led.
**Best for**: scrappy DTC brands, promos needing urgency through
roughness, retargeting fatigued audiences. **Tone**: scrappy,
self-aware, underdog.

## Post-it
Short message on a vivid post-it (yellow, pink, orange, lime), placed
near or on the product. Marker handwriting. Insider tone ("Pssst...",
"Don't tell anyone"). Often a promo code or short claim.
**Best for**: promo codes as insider tips, conversational
testimonials, multi-post-it comparisons. **Tone**: complicit,
"between you and me."

# Textual

Text dominates the frame — not as a supporting headline but as the
main content. Counter-intuitive on a visual platform but works
because it stands out from image-heavy feeds.

## Long Format
Text-heavy ad mimicking a letter, advertorial, editorial, press
release. Headline + 3-6 short paragraphs + signature at the bottom.
Clean white/beige background, serif or simple sans-serif. Product
small or absent. 80-200 words ideal.
**Best for**: founder-led announcements, closing letters, price
updates, manifestos, high-consideration purchases. **Tone**: candid,
sincere, considered.

## Native Ads (FB / Insta)
Mimics organic social content — IG story (orange/pink gradient), FB
post, DM thread, story sticker, Twitter post. Includes platform
chrome (status bar, time, profile pic). Casual lowercase voice with
emojis. UGC-style attached photo.
**Best for**: cold traffic fatigued on polished ads, peer-recommendation
products, Gen Z / millennials. **Tone**: organic, peer-to-peer.

# Headline

A short bold piece of copy carries the entire ad. Headline IS the
creative. Strong headline can stop the scroll in <500ms. Weak
headline kills the entire ad.

## Question
Direct question to the viewer ("you", "tu", "ton") targeting a
frustration, curiosity, or self-doubt. Brain can't help but try to
answer. Always second person, 4-12 words ideal. Tiny answer line
optional.
**Best for**: cold traffic with a recognizable frustration,
retargeting with a reframe. **Tone**: provocative, direct, "I'm
talking to YOU."

## Urgency
Headline screams scarcity, deadline, finality, stock pressure.
Loss aversion is the entire mechanism. All caps ("DERNIÈRE CHANCE",
"NOUS FERMONS", "TOUT DOIT PARTIR"). Aggressive palette: red,
black, neon yellow. Specific time/quantity for credibility.
**Best for**: scaling during sale periods, retargeting warm audiences,
genuine clearance. **Tone**: urgent, alarming.

## Classic 2
Polished product shot + bold PROMISE-driven headline. Different from
"Classic" (Annotated Product family): here the headline carries a
clear claim, product backs it. "Adieu / Plus jamais / Enfin / La
solution" framings common. Trust signals around (badges, ratings).
**Best for**: cold traffic needing immediate context, supplements,
beauty, wellness with clear outcomes. **Tone**: confident, polished.

## Humor
Headline (and visual) built around a joke, pun, absurd metaphor, or
visual gag. Viewer stops scrolling because of surprise, not
persuasion. Friendly typography, bright clean colors. Self-deprecation
or cheeky framing of taboo topics works well.
**Best for**: casual brand identities, taboo categories (intimate
hygiene, body hair), pattern-interrupt in serious feeds. **Tone**:
playful, irreverent. **Avoid**: medical/financial categories needing
gravitas.

# Other

Distinctive visual codes that don't share a common lever — each
borrows authority/familiarity from outside advertising (Google,
iPhone, press, statistics, illustration, typographic technique).

## Strikethrough (Phrase barrée)
Text visibly STRUCK THROUGH with a horizontal line, dramatizing a
transformation, objection, or price drop. Old text → new text in
larger / colored type. Stack of crossed objections ending in a
positive claim is a common pattern.
**Best for**: pricing creatives, objection-handling, "we're not like
the others" positioning. **Tone**: corrective, transformative.

## Google
Mimics a Google search results page: search bar with typed query,
results below, blue underlined links, Google logo. The query is the
hook (pain point or "how to..."). Brand appears in results /
featured snippet. Mobile or desktop UI.
**Best for**: implying organic demand, "the internet has decided"
positioning, retargeting research-mode users. **Tone**: authoritative,
organic-discovery.

## Statistics
Big bold percentages or numbers as the visual centerpiece ("84%
réduction", "92% reported", "98.6% effective"). Each stat paired
with a short label. Source line in small text for credibility. Often
paired with FDA/GMP/CE badges.
**Best for**: clinically-backed products, evidence-driven categories,
older / educated audiences. **Tone**: scientific, evidence-based.

## Drawing
Main visual is an ILLUSTRATION rather than a photograph — vector,
hand-drawn, anatomy diagram, cartoon, infographic. Used for
sensitive topics (intimate hygiene, organs), to simplify mechanisms,
or to bring friendly approachable tone.
**Best for**: anatomy / health topics, complex products needing
explanation, taboo subjects, friendly brand identities. **Tone**:
educational, friendly, simplifying.

## Press / Fake news / Blog
Mimics a press article, magazine column, blog post, or editorial.
Serif typography, bold editorial headline, byline + date, photo at
top, body paragraphs, "Lire plus" CTA. Borrows authority from the
form of journalism itself.
**Best for**: high-trust categories (health, wellness), audiences
distrusting traditional ads, products needing context. **Tone**:
editorial, journalistic.

## iPhone
Mimics iOS native UI — Notes app (yellow accent), Messages bubbles,
AirDrop popup, notification banner, lock screen. Includes iOS chrome
(status bar, time, battery). Casual mobile-typed voice. Product as
attached photo or absent.
**Best for**: peer-to-peer recommendation framings, mobile-first
audiences, "insider tip" framing. **Tone**: casual, off-the-record.

# Seasonal

Static ad anchored to a specific time-based event — a holiday, sales
period, or cultural moment. The event's visual codes (colors,
symbols, typography, motifs) wrap around the product. Borrows the
event's built-in mental context.

PARAMETERIZED: the specific event (Black Friday, Christmas,
Halloween, Mother's Day, French Days, Valentine's, Summer Sales,
New Year, Easter, Back to School, etc.) is specified by the user
at click-time. There is no closed list — apply general knowledge of
the event.

**Visual codes are dictated by the event**:
- Christmas: red, green, gold, snowflakes, gift boxes, Santa hats
- Halloween: orange, black, purple, jack-o-lanterns, bats, spiderwebs
- Valentine's: red, pink, hearts
- Black Friday: black, gold, neon yellow, bold sans-serif
- Easter: pastels, soft pink/yellow, eggs, bunnies
- Mother's Day: soft pinks, florals, tender register
- French Days: navy / white / red, French motifs
- New Year: gold, fireworks, midnight blue
- Summer Sales: bright blues / yellows, beach motifs

**Offer almost always rides along**: holiday-specific discount %,
event-tied promo code (XMAS10, BF50, MOM20), gift-framing ("perfect
gift for X"), date-bound urgency ("until midnight Dec 25").

**Copy register matches the event's emotion**: warmth/family for
Christmas, urgency/savings for Black Friday, playful spookiness for
Halloween, romance for Valentine's, gratitude for Mother's Day,
fresh-start for New Year.

**Combinable with any other family**: Christmas-themed Social Proof,
Black Friday Headline, Valentine's Handwriting. Seasonal is the
"skin", another family is the "structure."

**Best for**: every brand running offers around major calendar
events. **Tone**: dictated by the event itself.
**Avoid**: events the brand can't credibly participate in
(luxury + aggressive Black Friday damages positioning), events
the audience doesn't celebrate, generic "holiday" framing when a
specific event would land harder.
